What is Cybersecurity? It is the practice of defending systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks. These cyberattacks are usually aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information.

Types of Cyber Attacks

Phishing

PRIMARY THREAT VECTOR: 91% of Attacks Start with Email!

Phishing remains the most prevalent cyber threat. It only takes one click on a malicious link to compromise an entire network.

How Does Phishing Work?

Attackers impersonate trusted entities (like banks, executives, or IT support) to trick victims into revealing sensitive data, such as login credentials or credit card numbers.

Common Phishing Methods

Spear Phishing:

Highly personalized attack targeting specific individuals.

Whaling:

Targets high-profile executives (CEOs, CFOs).

Vishing:

Phishing conducted via voice calls (VOIP/phone).

Smishing:

Phishing conducted through SMS text messages.

Malware

DEFINITION: Any software designed to cause damage or gain unauthorized access.

Malware is often hidden inside legitimate files, attachments, or exploited vulnerabilities. Its primary goal is data theft, system disruption, or financial extortion.

Infection Vectors

The most common methods for spreading malware include malicious email attachments, compromised websites (drive-by downloads), and infected USB drives.

Major Types of Malware

Virus:

Needs a host file to replicate.

Worm:

Spreads independently across networks.

Trojan Horse:

Disguised as legitimate software.

Ransomware:

Encrypts files, demands ransom.

Spyware:

Monitors user activity secretly.

DDOS Attacks

GOAL: Make an online service unavailable by overwhelming it with traffic.

Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) uses multiple compromised computer systems (Botnets) to flood a target, forcing it to slow down or crash entirely.

The Impact

DDoS attacks cause massive financial losses due to downtime, damage customer trust, and can be used as a distraction for more sophisticated breaches.

Categorizing DDoS Attacks

Volumetric:

Consumes all available bandwidth.

Protocol:

Exploits server resources (e.g., SYN Flood).

Application Layer:

Targets specific application functions (Layer 7).

Social Engineering

FOCUS: Exploiting human psychology and trust, not system vulnerabilities.

This is the art of manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information, often bypassing technical defenses entirely.

The Tactics

Attackers use urgency, authority (impersonating a CEO or IT admin), or curiosity to compel a victim to act quickly before thinking critically.

Common Social Engineering Techniques

Pretexting:

Creating a false scenario (pretext) to obtain information.

Tailgating:

Gaining physical access by following authorized personnel.

Baiting:

Offering a reward (like a free movie download or infected USB drive).

Impersonation:

Pretending to be a trusted figure over the phone or email.

Zero-Day Exploits

DEFINITION: An attack that exploits a software vulnerability before the vendor knows about it.

"Zero-Day" refers to the fact that the vendor has "zero days" to fix the flaw because it is already being actively exploited in the wild.

Defense Challenge

Since there is no patch available, traditional signature-based security tools cannot detect or prevent these attacks, making them extremely valuable to sophisticated threat actors.

Mitigation and Prevention

Behavioral Detection:

Monitoring system activity for unusual patterns.

Segmentation:

Limiting lateral movement across the network.

Least Privilege:

Granting minimal user access rights.

Virtual Patching:

Implementing security controls before official patches.
